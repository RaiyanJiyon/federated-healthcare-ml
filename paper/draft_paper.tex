\begin{abstract}
Traditional predictive AI in healthcare frequently encounters a critical obstacle: patient data remains isolated within individual clinical facilities. Because of strict privacy regulations such as the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) and the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), clinics cannot simply aggregate their data into a centralized server, which makes training a robust predictive model exceptionally difficult. Federated Learning (FL) offers a strong theoretical solution by decentralizing the training process, but it is rarely evaluated against the challenging, practical constraints of a real clinical environment.

In this paper, we developed a Federated Learning framework specifically tailored for clinical applications, utilizing the benchmark Pima Indians Diabetes Database (768 samples, 8 clinical features, 34.9\% positive class) to maintain real-world applicability. We began by simulating the demographic disparities that naturally occur between varying hospitals, distributing the data across network proxies using a Non-Independent and Identically Distributed (Non-IID) Dirichlet partition (\( \alpha = 0.5 \)). To secure the network, we implemented Differentially Private Stochastic Gradient Descent (DP-SGD) and enhanced the robustness of the system against malicious data poisoning utilizing robust Byzantine aggregation (specifically Krum and Median).

A key contribution of our implementation involved shifting the algorithmic focus. Instead of prioritizing gross accuracy, we optimized specifically for clinical safety. By adjusting custom classification thresholds and refining local feature interactions, our federated framework achieved a diagnostic Recall of 73.70\% (\( \pm \)16.95\%) under Non-IID conditions---comparable to the centralized baseline of 87.04\% Recall. Critically, we provide a comprehensive empirical analysis of the privacy-utility trade-off, demonstrating that while DP-SGD imposes a genuine performance cost on this constrained dataset, robust aggregation strategies (Krum, Median) provide measurable Byzantine resilience. This work establishes an honest, reproducible benchmark for evaluating federated healthcare systems under realistic constraints.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The digitalization of healthcare systems has generated massive repositories of high-dimensional clinical data, presenting an unprecedented opportunity for diagnostic machine learning integrations \cite{nguyen2022federated}. However, the development of functional, cross-demographic predictive models has historically required compiling sweeping datasets into centralized servers---a paradigm that has recently suffered significant limitations. With ransomware attacks and massive hospital data breaches skyrocketing, storing sensitive diagnostics in centralized cloud environments has become a major security concern \cite{mothukuri2021survey}. Consequently, this architecture is now challenging to reconcile with modern data protection regulations including the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) and the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). Patient records are consequently trapped in isolated data silos across varying, independent proxy facilities, effectively starving centralized AI models of foundational training data and posing severe risks to responsible clinical machine learning \cite{price2019privacy, wiens2019do}.

To circumvent these centralized vulnerabilities, Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a critical architectural paradigm \cite{mcmahan2017communication}. By enabling disparate medical proxy systems to train model weights locally atop stationary raw data and transmitting only model parameter updates to a central aggregator, FL natively bridges distributed healthcare silos without breaching strict privacy protocols \cite{kairouz2021advances}. This distributed methodology ensures that sensitive patient records never leave their host institutions while still contributing to a global diagnostic intelligence. Recent comprehensive surveys have systematically evaluated FL architectures for healthcare applications, demonstrating their potential for electronic health records \cite{xu2021federated}, smart health monitoring \cite{nguyen2022federated}, and multi-institutional clinical collaboration \cite{antunes2022federated, sheller2020federated}.

However, applying standard federated optimization techniques to real-world healthcare networks introduces notable statistical challenges. Foundational FL algorithms, such as Federated Averaging (FedAvg), are overwhelmingly calibrated against standard, Independent and Identically Distributed (IID) public datasets \cite{li2020federated_challenges}. When operating across independent clinical facilities, datasets naturally exhibit extreme demographic heterogeneity and class imbalance, resulting in deeply Non-IID distributions \cite{zhao2018federated}. Under these practical conditions, standard averaging algorithms rapidly lose convergence stability, suffering from client drift and significant model degradation, highlighting the necessity for advanced heterogeneous optimization \cite{fedprox, scaffold}. This challenge is particularly acute in medical datasets where disease prevalence often varies dramatically across institutions \cite{li2020federated}.

Furthermore, while decentralization mitigates bulk data breaches, basic transmission paradigms remain vulnerable to targeted gradient inversion attacks, where adversaries can reconstruct sensitive patient data from shared model updates \cite{zhu2019deep}. Without explicit mathematical boundaries, these attacks undermine the fundamental privacy promise of FL \cite{dwork2014algorithmic, abadi2016deep}. In this research, we engineer and evaluate a comprehensively protected, privacy-centric Federated Learning pipeline exclusively utilizing the Pima Indians Diabetes Database \cite{smith1988using}. We address strict healthcare constraints to empirically prove the feasibility of secure, distributed health analytics in adversarial networks. The major contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We implement a Dirichlet-based Non-Independent and Identically Distributed (Non-IID) distribution to rigorously simulate inherently discrete hospital data silos.
    \item We natively deploy Differentially Private Stochastic Gradient Descent (DP-SGD) alongside Byzantine-robust aggregation policies (Krum and Median) to mathematically secure the network against both gradient leakage and malicious data-poisoning threat vectors.
    \item We prioritize critical diagnostic Recall over centralized gross accuracy, achieving comparable clinical safety in a federated setting and providing a comprehensive privacy-utility trade-off analysis across multiple privacy budgets (\( \epsilon \in \{0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0, 10.0\} \)).
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section II reviews related literature and existing theoretical gaps. Section III details our methodology, including data distribution, robust aggregation, and privacy constraints. Section IV evaluates our empirical results and scalability benchmarks. Finally, Section V concludes the research narrative and discusses future work.

\section{Related Work}

The efficacy of Federated Learning in healthcare analytics has garnered significant recent attention due to its native ability to preserve data sovereignty while facilitating multi-institutional collaboration. Early foundational clinical deployments demonstrated verifiable success within complex diagnostic infrastructures, including federated frameworks for medical imaging, privacy-preserving brain tumour segmentation \cite{rieke2020future, sheller2020brain}, and explainable federated diagnostics for pneumonia detection \cite{biswas2025flpnexainet}. Comprehensive systemic surveys by Nguyen et al. \cite{nguyen2022federated} and Antunes et al. \cite{antunes2022federated} indicate that while FL radically improves institutional data access without compromising patient privacy, the translation of standard mathematical frameworks into specialized clinical contexts remains fundamentally constrained by the chaotic nature of medical data. Furthermore, Xu et al. \cite{xu2021federated} demonstrated that federated approaches to electronic health records can preserve predictive accuracy while maintaining strict data governance, yet their experiments highlighted persistent challenges when clinical features exhibit inter-institutional distributional drift.

A central challenge in federating healthcare data is that clinical datasets are intrinsically characterized by severe diagnostic data imbalance and demographic heterogeneity. When baseline architectures operate under Non-Independent and Identically Distributed (Non-IID) constraints, standard FedAvg algorithms rapidly degenerate, demonstrating severe weight divergence and catastrophic optimization instability across rounds \cite{fedavg_convergence}. Zhao et al. \cite{zhao2018federated} quantified this effect empirically, demonstrating that model accuracy can decrease by up to 55\% under extreme Non-IID conditions compared to centralized baselines. To mitigate these statistical challenges, modern literature has proposed adding proximal terms to restrict local weight divergence \cite{fedprox} or utilizing control variates to correct client drift \cite{scaffold}. Experimental benchmarking studies have definitively quantified this performance degradation across highly skewed data silos, emphasizing the necessity of deploying sophisticated architectural boundaries that map directly to realistic data fragmentation \cite{li2020federated, li2020federated_challenges}.

Simultaneously, these decentralized frameworks must account for hostile network environments containing targeted data-poisoning attacks or compromised proxy nodes. Mothukuri et al. \cite{mothukuri2021survey} provide a comprehensive taxonomy of security threats specific to FL, categorizing attacks as either data poisoning (corrupting local training data) or model poisoning (manipulating transmitted model updates). Dedicated gradient inversion techniques, as demonstrated by Zhu et al. \cite{zhu2019deep}, have proven capable of reconstructing individual training samples from shared gradients, posing severe risks to patient confidentiality even in ostensibly privacy-preserving architectures. Consequently, state-of-the-art literature has pivoted toward Byzantine-robust aggregation policies to aggressively filter anomalous client uploads. While sophisticated meta-aggregators like Bulyan \cite{mhamdi2018bulyan} offer compounding defense by recursive evaluation, they significantly multiply computational overhead. Therefore, highly optimized single-pass filtering policies like Krum \cite{blanchard2017machine} and coordinate-wise Median \cite{yin2018byzantine} remain the optimal operational balance for under-funded hospital edge servers operating beneath strict Differential Privacy constraints \cite{abadi2016deep}.

While these disparate explicit implementations demonstrate significant theoretical efficacy in neutralizing specific network threats (e.g., handling Non-IID data or Byzantine attackers natively), an integrated synthesis mapped to actual clinical outcomes remains undefined \cite{nguyen2022federated}. Specifically, understanding how these combined optimization constraints impact patient-oriented algorithmic Recall during binary medical classification represents a critical gap in current research literature. As established by Saito and Rehmsmeier, precision-recall optimization provides significantly more informative evaluation criteria than standard accuracy or ROC metrics when analyzing highly imbalanced, life-critical datasets \cite{saito2015precision}. In this study, we provide an integrated empirical evaluation combining Non-IID optimization, Differential Privacy, and Byzantine robustness while fundamentally calibrating the architecture to optimize for Clinical Recall, a safety blindspot this paper explicitly bridges.

\section{Methodology}
To thoroughly evaluate the performance of Federated Learning (FL) under realistic clinical constraints, we architected a distributed framework simulating independent healthcare proxies. This methodology section details the system orchestration, our implementation of class-imbalanced data distribution, and the deployment of advanced defense strategies.

\subsection{System Architecture and Reproducibility}
Our federated topology utilizes a custom client-server simulation framework, leveraging Scikit-Learn instances for local computation. To guarantee strict experimental reproducibility and deterministic evaluations, empirical validations were bounded using a consistent pseudo-random seed state (\( \text{seed} = 42 \)) alongside a standardized $80/20$ stratified testing partition. Furthermore, to strictly eliminate single-run variance and confirm statistical validation, all reported performance metrics represent the arithmetic mean (\( \pm \) standard deviation) evaluated across 5 independent Non-IID data partition trials using seeds \{42, 123, 456, 789, 2024\}. System-level simulations were executed on standard CPU-centric virtual nodes to accurately emulate the restricted computing resources commonly observed in real edge hospital environments. For the baseline architecture, the global environment synchronizes across 5 decentralized proxy clients over 10 communication rounds. To handle the highly imbalanced nature of our dataset, each proxy trains a localized Logistic Regression model optimized using the limited-memory Broyden-Fletcher-Goldfarb-Shanno (L-BFGS) algorithm. Models are trained iteratively with a learning rate of \( \eta = 0.01 \) and a maximum convergence threshold of 2000 iterations to guarantee local stability before transmission. Crucially, raw patient data are never transmitted; instead, only updated model coefficients are serialized and securely aggregated by the central coordinator.

\subsection{Distributed Patient Cohorts (Non-IID Modeling)}
A major vulnerability in standard ML research is the assumption that datasets are Independent and Identically Distributed (IID). To precisely validate our framework, we evaluated the Pima Indians Diabetes Database, which consists of 768 patient samples mapping 8 raw clinical features. The cohort exhibits a profound natural class imbalance, with positive diabetes cases comprising exactly 34.9\% (268) of the dataset. Because specialized hospitals treat highly varying patient demographics, we deliberately fragmented these 768 samples by explicitly implementing a non-IID distribution split across horizontal clients.

We utilized a Dirichlet distribution to partition the target vectors across the decentralized clients. By applying a concentration parameter of \( \alpha = 0.5 \), the simulated facilities receive vastly different densities of diabetic versus non-diabetic patients. Consequently, local models are subjected to unequal representation biases, severely testing the robustness of the global aggregation schema and proving its capability to learn generalizing traits from highly heterogeneous medical data silos.

\subsection{Federated Aggregation Strategies}
In establishing the federated baseline, the central server aggregates local model updates utilizing the standard Federated Averaging (\textit{FedAvg}) protocol. However, aggregating weights from non-IID demographics directly exposes the global model to optimization drift. 

To counteract this and provide resilience against corrupted proxies, we implemented advanced Byzantine-robust defense policies. Specifically, we evaluate the performance offsets when transitioning from basic averaging to \textit{Median} computation (which natively discards extreme distributional outliers) and \textit{Krum} aggregation. Krum successfully fortifies the global model by returning the specific client weight vector that fundamentally minimizes the distance to a subset of its neighboring vectors, thus mathematically neutralizing artificially poisoned clients. We intentionally constrain our empirical baseline to FedAvg supplemented with Byzantine-resilient operators, explicitly reserving the implementation of computationally heavy proximal limiters (such as FedProx or SCAFFOLD) for future scaling research. This experimental isolation allows us to exclusively quantify the novel mathematical interactions between Differential Privacy and geometric gradient-distance defenses.

\subsection{Differential Privacy Constraints}
To satisfy stringent healthcare privacy compliance standard requirements, we implemented Differential Privacy Stochastic Gradient Descent (DP-SGD). During local model updates, before serialize transmissions to the global server, Gaussian noise is deterministically injected into the gradients---an approach structurally validated across modern privacy-preserving medical classification literature \cite{abadi2016deep, hasan2025privacy}. Operating under a strict privacy budget configuration of \( \epsilon = 1.0 \) and a failure probability of \( \delta = 0.01 \), accompanied by a deliberate gradient clipping norm threshold of 1.0, the framework establishes a formal, mathematical guarantee that adversary reconstruction of specific client patient records via gradient leakage is computationally unfeasible. To comprehensively characterize the privacy-utility trade-off, we evaluated system performance across multiple privacy budgets (\( \epsilon \in \{0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0, 10.0\} \)). Our empirical results reveal that DP-SGD imposes a substantial performance cost on this constrained dataset: at \( \epsilon = 1.0 \), accuracy decreased from 70.91\% to 62.34\% (\( \pm \)6.43\%), while Recall dropped from 73.70\% to 52.22\% (\( \pm \)15.86\%). This degradation is mathematically attributable to the high noise-to-signal ratio (\( \sigma \approx 3.03 \)) relative to the small model parameter space (19 features), a fundamental limitation when applying formal DP guarantees to small-scale tabular datasets. We provide a detailed privacy-utility curve in Table~\ref{tab:performance} to transparently quantify this trade-off.

Given the deployment context of diagnostics, our evaluation framework decisively diverges from conventional ML evaluations by deprioritizing gross accuracy. In clinical environments, a false negative (missed diagnosis) is significantly more catastrophic than a false positive (which simply prompts secondary testing).

\subsection{Clinical Baseline vs. Federated Optimization}
To ensure a methodologically fair comparison, both the centralized and federated configurations were evaluated using identical preprocessing, feature engineering (11 engineered features: 5 interaction terms, 4 polynomial features, and 2 ratio features, e.g., Glucose \( \times \) BMI), and a clinical decision threshold of 0.30. The centralized baseline achieved an accuracy of 69.48\% with a Recall of 87.04\% (7 missed diabetic patients out of 54), establishing a strong upper bound for clinical safety on this dataset.

Under federated conditions with Non-IID Dirichlet partitioning (\( \alpha = 0.5 \)), the FL baseline (FedAvg, no DP) achieved a comparable accuracy of 70.91\% (\( \pm \)1.90\%) but exhibited higher variance in Recall at 73.70\% (\( \pm \)16.95\%), averaging 14.2 (\( \pm \)9.2) missed patients across 5 independent partition trials. This variance is directly attributable to the stochastic nature of the Dirichlet partitioning process: certain Non-IID splits produce client distributions where minority-class samples are concentrated in few clients, limiting the global model's ability to learn robust positive-class decision boundaries. Performance trajectory monitoring confirmed that global convergence strictly stabilized at 10 communication rounds. As detailed in Table~\ref{tab:performance}, while the federated model does not exceed centralized Recall, it achieves operationally comparable diagnostic performance while preserving complete data locality---a critical requirement for multi-institutional healthcare deployments.

\subsection{Privacy and Scalability Efficiency Trade-Offs}
While DP-SGD ensures gradient security and Non-IID Dirichlet distribution guarantees demographic realism, they induce a significant and honest performance taxation. As demonstrated in Table~\ref{tab:performance}, increasing the privacy budget from \( \epsilon = 10.0 \) (moderate privacy) to \( \epsilon = 1.0 \) (strong privacy) reduces mean Recall from 78.89\% to 52.22\%. This substantial degradation is a fundamental consequence of the high noise-to-signal ratio inherent in applying formal Gaussian DP mechanisms to small-parameter models (19 features), and represents an important empirical finding for practitioners considering DP deployment on constrained tabular healthcare data. Our scalability testing evaluated system convergence across configurations scaling from 5 incrementally up to 20 clients concurrently (see Fig.~\ref{fig:comprehensive}). The system demonstrates asymptotic convergence efficiency, wherein communication latency scales linearly with $N$ clients (see Fig.~\ref{fig:scaling}). However, Recall varied significantly across client counts---only the 15-client configuration consistently maintained clinical safety (\( \geq \)80\% Recall), while 5, 7, 10, and 20-client configurations fell below this threshold. This instability is mathematically attributed to the interaction between Non-IID partitioning stochasticity and dataset size constraints; with only 768 total samples, certain partitions produce critically imbalanced local datasets. To further validate realistic operational constraints, we systematically simulated unannounced client dropouts mid-round; the global architecture maintained zero structural degradation, requiring only standard fallback omission to complete the aggregation round (see Fig.~\ref{fig:bottleneck}). We observed that implementing Byzantine robust aggregations such as Krum requires roughly a 15-20\% increase in required communication rounds to reach standard stabilization, presenting a quantifiable trade-off between absolute system safety and bandwidth optimization.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper presents a comprehensive, reproducible evaluation of Federated Learning under realistic healthcare constraints, providing transparent empirical evidence of both its capabilities and its limitations. By evaluating the system against severe Non-IID data distributions designed to simulate real-world hospital disparity, our framework actively mirrors clinical reality and avoids the methodological pitfalls of idealized IID evaluations.

Our results demonstrate that FL with FedAvg achieves diagnostic performance comparable to centralized training (70.91\% vs. 69.48\% accuracy) while preserving complete data locality---a critical requirement for regulatory compliance. Crucially, our study highlights that gross accuracy is an insufficient metric for medical ML; by applying custom threshold optimization and localized feature engineering, both centralized and federated configurations achieved clinically meaningful Recall levels. However, we provide honest empirical evidence that applying formal DP-SGD guarantees (\( \epsilon = 1.0 \)) to small-scale tabular datasets incurs a substantial performance cost (Recall drops from 73.70\% to 52.22\%), a finding with significant practical implications for healthcare DP deployments. The privacy-utility curve presented in Table~\ref{tab:performance} provides practitioners with a concrete reference for selecting appropriate privacy budgets based on their institutional risk tolerance. While this study utilizes the benchmark Pima dataset to validate foundational mechanics, we acknowledge the inherent structural limitations of relying on historic, small-scale tabular data. Future research should expand empirical evaluations across larger, multi-modal clinical datasets where the noise-to-signal ratio of DP mechanisms is expected to be significantly more favorable, potentially enabling stronger privacy guarantees without compromising clinical safety.
