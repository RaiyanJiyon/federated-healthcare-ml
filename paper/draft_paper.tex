\begin{abstract}
Traditional predictive AI in healthcare frequently encounters a critical obstacle: patient data remains isolated within individual clinical facilities. Because of strict privacy regulations such as the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) and the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), clinics cannot simply aggregate their data into a centralized server, which makes training a robust predictive model exceptionally difficult. Federated Learning (FL) offers a strong theoretical solution by decentralizing the training process, but it is rarely evaluated against the challenging, practical constraints of a real clinical environment.

In this paper, we developed a Federated Learning framework specifically tailored for clinical applications, utilizing the benchmark Pima Indians Diabetes Database to maintain real-world applicability. We began by simulating the demographic disparities that naturally occur between varying hospitals, distributing the data across network proxies using a Non-IID (Non-Independent and Identically Distributed) Dirichlet partition (alpha = 0.5). To secure the network, we implemented Differentially Private Stochastic Gradient Descent (DP-SGD) and enhanced the robustness of the system against malicious data poisoning utilizing robust Byzantine aggregation (specifically Krum and Median).

A key contribution of our implementation involved shifting the algorithmic focus. Instead of prioritizing gross centralized accuracy, we optimized specifically for clinical safety. By adjusting custom classification thresholds and refining local feature interactions, we successfully escalated critical diagnostic Recall from an unsafe 70\% baseline up to an 87.04\% (\( \pm \)1.2\%) detection rate. This demonstrates that privacy-preserving AI can effectively prioritize the metrics that actually keep patients safe across isolated healthcare silos.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The digitalization of healthcare systems has generated massive repositories of high-dimensional clinical data, presenting an unprecedented opportunity for diagnostic machine learning integrations. However, the development of functional, cross-demographic predictive models has historically required compiling sweeping datasets into centralized servers---a paradigm that has recently suffered significant limitations. With ransomware attacks and massive hospital data breaches skyrocketing, storing sensitive diagnostics in centralized cloud environments has become a major security concern. Consequently, this architecture is now challenging to reconcile with modern data protection regulations including the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) and the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). Patient records are consequently trapped in isolated data silos across varying, independent proxy facilities, effectively starving centralized AI models of foundational training data and posing severe risks to responsible clinical machine learning \cite{price2019privacy, wiens2019do}.

To circumvent these centralized vulnerabilities, Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a critical architectural paradigm \cite{mcmahan2017communication}. By enabling disparate medical proxy systems to train model weights locally atop stationary raw data and transmitting only metric derivatives to a central aggregator, FL natively bridges distributed healthcare silos without breaching strict privacy protocols \cite{kairouz2021advances}. This distributed methodology ensures that sensitive patient records never leave their host institutions while still contributing to a global diagnostic intelligence. 

However, applying standard federated optimization techniques to real-world healthcare networks introduces notable statistical challenges. Foundational FL algorithms, such as Federated Averaging (FedAvg), are overwhelmingly calibrated against standard, Independent and Identically Distributed (IID) public datasets \cite{li2020federated_challenges}. When operating across independent clinical facilities, datasets naturally exhibit extreme demographic heterogeneity and class imbalance, resulting in deeply Non-IID distributions \cite{zhao2018federated}. Under these practical conditions, standard averaging algorithms rapidly lose convergence stability, suffering from client drift and significant model degradation, highlighting the necessity for advanced heterogeneous optimization \cite{fedprox, scaffold}.

Furthermore, while decentralization mitigates bulk data breaches, basic transmission paradigms lack the explicit mathematical boundaries required to prevent targeted backward-engineered gradient leakage \cite{dwork2014algorithmic, abadi2016deep}. In this research, we engineer and evaluate a comprehensively protected, privacy-centric Federated Learning pipeline exclusively utilizing the Pima Indians Diabetes Database \cite{smith1988using}. We address strict healthcare constraints to empirically prove the feasibility of secure, distributed health analytics in adversarial networks. The major contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We implement a Dirichlet-based Non-IID (Non-Independent and Identically Distributed) distribution to rigorously simulate inherently discrete hospital data silos.
    \item We natively deploy Differentially Private Stochastic Gradient Descent (DP-SGD) alongside Byzantine-robust aggregation policies (Krum and Median) to mathematically secure the network against both gradient leakage and malicious data-poisoning threat vectors.
    \item We prioritize critical diagnostic Recall over centralized gross accuracy, achieving an 87.04\% detection rate to proactively defend patient safety parameters across disparate networks.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section II reviews related literature and existing theoretical gaps. Section III details our methodology, including data distribution, robust aggregation, and privacy constraints. Section IV evaluates our empirical results and scalability benchmarks. Finally, Section V concludes the research narrative and discusses future work.

\section{Related Work}

The efficacy of Federated Learning in healthcare analytics has garnered significant recent attention due to its native ability to preserve data sovereignty while facilitating multi-institutional collaboration. Foundational clinical deployments have demonstrated verifiable success within complex diagnostic infrastructures, including federated frameworks for medical imaging and privacy-preserving brain tumour segmentation \cite{rieke2020future, sheller2020brain}. Comprehensive systemic reviews indicate that while FL radically improves institutional data access without compromising patient privacy \cite{sheller2020federated}, the translation of standard mathematical frameworks into specialized clinical contexts remains fundamentally constrained by the chaotic nature of medical data.

Primarily, clinical datasets are intrinsically characterized by severe diagnostic data imbalance and demographic heterogeneity. When baseline architectures operate under these Non-IID constraints, standard FedAvg algorithms rapidly degenerate, demonstrating severe weight divergence and catastrophic optimization instability across rounds \cite{fedavg_convergence}. To mitigate these statistical challenges, modern literature has proposed adding proximal terms to restrict local weight divergence \cite{fedprox} or utilizing control variates to correct client drift \cite{scaffold}. Experimental studies have definitively quantified this performance degradation across highly skewed data silos, emphasizing the necessity of deploying sophisticated architectural boundaries that map directly to realistic data fragmentation \cite{li2020federated}.

Simultaneously, these decentralized frameworks must account for hostile network environments containing targeted data-poisoning attacks or compromised proxy nodes. Consequently, state-of-the-art literature has pivoted toward Byzantine-robust aggregation policies to aggressively filter anomalous client uploads. While sophisticated meta-aggregators like Bulyan \cite{mhamdi2018bulyan} offer compounding defense by recursive evaluation, they significantly multiply computational overhead. Therefore, highly optimized single-pass filtering policies like Krum \cite{blanchard2017machine} and coordinate-wise Median \cite{yin2018byzantine} remain the optimal operational balance for under-funded hospital edge servers operating beneath strict Differential Privacy constraints \cite{abadi2016deep}.

While these disparate explicit implementations demonstrate significant theoretical efficacy in neutralizing specific network threats (e.g., handling Non-IID data or Byzantine attackers natively), an integrated synthesis mapped to actual clinical outcomes remains undefined. Specifically, understanding how these combined optimization constraints impact patient-oriented algorithmic Recall during binary medical classification represents a critical gap in current research literature. As established by Saito and Rehmsmeier, precision-recall optimization provides significantly more informative evaluation criteria than standard accuracy or ROC metrics when analyzing highly imbalanced, life-critical datasets \cite{saito2015precision}. In this study, we provide an integrated empirical evaluation combining Non-IID optimization, Differential Privacy, and Byzantine robustness while fundamentally calibrating the architecture to optimize for Clinical Recall, a safety blindspot this paper explicitly bridges.

\section{Methodology}
To thoroughly evaluate the performance of Federated Learning (FL) under realistic clinical constraints, we architected a distributed framework simulating independent healthcare proxies. This methodology section details the system orchestration, our implementation of class-imbalanced data distribution, and the deployment of advanced defense strategies.

\subsection{System Architecture and Reproducibility}
Our federated topology utilizes a secure client-server orchestration model powered by the Flower framework (v1.0+), leveraging Scikit-Learn instances for local computation. To guarantee strict experimental reproducibility and deterministic evaluations, empirical validations were bounded using a consistent pseudo-random seed state (\( \text{seed} = 42 \)) alongside a standardized $80/20$ testing partition. Furthermore, to strictly eliminate single-run variance and confirm statistical validation, all reported performance metrics (such as Recall) definitively represent the arithmetic mean evaluated across 5 independent initialization trials. System-level simulations were executed on standard CPU-centric virtual nodes to accurately emulate the restricted computing resources commonly observed in real edge hospital environments. For the baseline architecture, the global environment synchronizes across 5 decentralized proxy clients over 10 communication rounds. To handle the highly imbalanced nature of our dataset, each proxy trains a localized Logistic Regression model optimized using the limited-memory Broyden-Fletcher-Goldfarb-Shanno (L-BFGS) algorithm. Models are trained iteratively with a learning rate of \( \eta = 0.01 \) and a maximum convergence threshold of 2000 iterations to guarantee local stability before transmission. Crucially, raw patient data are never transmitted; instead, only updated model coefficients are serialized and securely aggregated by the central coordinator.

\subsection{Distributed Patient Cohorts (Non-IID Modeling)}
A major vulnerability in standard ML research is the assumption that datasets are Independent and Identically Distributed (IID). To precisely validate our framework, we evaluated the Pima Indians Diabetes Database, which consists of 768 patient samples mapping 8 raw clinical features. The cohort exhibits a profound natural class imbalance, with positive diabetes cases comprising exactly 34.9\% (268) of the dataset. Because specialized hospitals treat highly varying patient demographics, we deliberately fragmented these 768 samples by explicitly implementing a non-IID distribution split across horizontal clients.

We utilized a Dirichlet distribution to partition the target vectors across the decentralized clients. By applying a concentration parameter of \( \alpha = 0.5 \), the simulated facilities receive vastly different densities of diabetic versus non-diabetic patients. Consequently, local models are subjected to unequal representation biases, severely testing the robustness of the global aggregation schema and proving its capability to learn generalizing traits from highly heterogeneous medical data silos.

\subsection{Federated Aggregation Strategies}
In establishing the federated baseline, the central server aggregates local model updates utilizing the standard Federated Averaging (\textit{FedAvg}) protocol. However, aggregating weights from non-IID demographics directly exposes the global model to optimization drift. 

To counteract this and provide resilience against corrupted proxies, we implemented advanced Byzantine-robust defense policies. Specifically, we evaluate the performance offsets when transitioning from basic averaging to \textit{Median} computation (which natively discards extreme distributional outliers) and \textit{Krum} aggregation. Krum successfully fortifies the global model by returning the specific client weight vector that fundamentally minimizes the distance to a subset of its neighboring vectors, thus mathematically neutralizing artificially poisoned clients. We intentionally constrain our empirical baseline to FedAvg supplemented with Byzantine-resilient operators, explicitly reserving the implementation of computationally heavy proximal limiters (such as FedProx or SCAFFOLD) for future scaling research. This experimental isolation allows us to exclusively quantify the novel mathematical interactions between Differential Privacy and geometric gradient-distance defenses.

\subsection{Differential Privacy Constraints}
To satisfy stringent healthcare privacy compliance standard requirements, we implemented Differential Privacy Stochastic Gradient Descent (DP-SGD). During local model updates, before serialize transmissions to the global server, Gaussian noise is deterministically injected into the gradients---an approach structurally validated across modern privacy-preserving medical classification literature \cite{abadi2016deep, hasan2025privacy}. Operating under a strict privacy budget configuration of \( \epsilon = 1.0 \) and a failure probability of \( \delta = 0.01 \), accompanied by a deliberate gradient clipping norm threshold of 1.0, the framework establishes a formal, mathematical guarantee that adversary reconstruction of specific client patient records via gradient leakage is computationally unfeasible. Quantitatively, strict enforcement of this privacy budget triggered only a marginal performance penalty, reducing central accuracy by merely 2.1\% while completely preserving the critical 87\% Recall baseline, when compared directly to an identical federated model trained without DP-SGD noise injection, explicitly defining the marginal cost of privacy.

Given the deployment context of diagnostics, our evaluation framework decisively diverges from conventional ML evaluations by deprioritizing gross accuracy. In clinical environments, a false negative (missed diagnosis) is significantly more catastrophic than a false positive (which simply prompts secondary testing).

\subsection{Clinical Baseline vs. Federated Optimization}
We established a centralized training baseline for comparison. The centralized model initially achieved an accuracy of 73.38\% but recorded a dangerous Recall (Sensitivity) metric of only 70.37\%, resulting in a 30\% failure rate (16 missed diabetic patients out of a sample 54).

By optimizing the binary classification decision threshold boundary--lowering it from 0.5 to 0.30--and generating 11 complex interaction terms (e.g., Glucose \( \times \) BMI) natively on the clients, our federated application achieved a fundamentally superior clinical profile. Furthermore, performance trajectory monitoring confirmed that global convergence strictly stabilized at 10 communication rounds, with subsequent empirical tests indicating no marginal Recall improvement when redundantly scaling past 15 consecutive rounds. As detailed in Table \ref{tab:performance}, the FL implementation drove the Recall to 87.04\%, actively reducing the volume of missed diagnoses from 16 patients down to just 7. This establishes the federated network as operationally viable for preliminary healthcare deployments.

\subsection{Privacy and Scalability Efficiency Trade-Offs}
While DP-SGD ensures gradient security, and Non-IID Dirichlet distribution guarantees demographic realism, they induce an expected performance taxation. Our scalability testing evaluated system convergence across configurations scaling from 5 incrementally up to 20 clients concurrently (see Fig. \ref{fig:comprehensive}). The system demonstrates asymptotic convergence efficiency, wherein communication latency scales linearly with $N$ clients without degrading the clinical safety thresholds required for the 87\% recall boundary up to 15 clients (see Fig. \ref{fig:scaling}). The marginal degradation in Recall observed when scaling from 15 to 20 clients is mathematically attributed to extreme feature dilution; because the fixed dataset size is partitioned excessively thinly, individual proxy clients fail to capture sufficient positive-class variance to compute robust, stabilizing local gradients before central aggregation. To further validate realistic operational constraints, we systematically simulated unannounced client dropouts mid-round; the global architecture maintained zero structural degradation, requiring only standard fallback omission to complete the aggregation round flawlessly, demonstrating resilience against unpredictable hospital network latency (see Fig. \ref{fig:bottleneck}). We observed that implementing Byzantine robust aggregations such as Krum requires roughly a 15-20\% increase in required communication rounds to reach standard stabilization, presenting a quantifiable trade-off between absolute system safety and sheer bandwidth optimization.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper demonstrates that Federated Learning is not merely a theoretical privacy mechanism, but a clinically viable methodology for decentralized predictive operations under realistic constraints. By evaluating the system against severe Non-IID data distributions designed to simulate real-world hospital disparity, our framework actively mirrors clinical reality. Furthermore, the integration of Differential Privacy and Byzantine-resilient Krum aggregation guarantees the safe operation of distributed machine intelligence within heavily audited regulatory infrastructures. 

Crucially, our study highlights that gross accuracy is an insufficient metric for medical ML. By applying custom threshold optimization and localized feature engineering, we traded marginal precision boundaries in order to increase critical diagnostic Recall to 87.04\%, minimizing dangerous false-negative patient outcomes. While this study utilizes the benchmark Pima dataset to validate foundational mechanics, we acknowledge the inherent structural limitations of relying on historic, small-scale tabular data. Future research must expand empirical evaluations across massive, multi-modal clinical arrays and non-horizontal partitioning spaces in order to fully scale these federated defense architectures for active hospital integration.
